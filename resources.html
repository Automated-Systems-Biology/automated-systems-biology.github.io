Phosphate Buffers

Description

Phosphate buffer is among the most used buffers in chemistry and biology, with a working range of pH 5.8–8. It is non-toxic to cells and the most physiological of common buffer salts, and its pH is stable with respect to temperature changes. However, it is prone to microbial contamination and, during fixation, is likely to cause precipitates — especially with uranyl acetate and lead salts. The saline-containing form (PBS / Dulbecco’s phosphate-buffered saline, DPBS) is an isotonic buffer frequently used in biological applications such as washing cells. Phosphate buffers bind divalent cations well and precipitate in the presence of alcohol.

Citrate Buffers

Description

Sodium citrate–citric acid buffer is an acidic buffering medium in the range of pH 3–6.2, used in biochemistry and molecular biology. It is widely used in RNA isolation as it prevents base hydrolysis; for the same reason saline-sodium citrate (SSC) buffer is widely used in in-situ hybridisation. For immunohistochemistry it is said to increase signal without increasing background.

Cacodylate Buffer

Description

Working range pH 5–7.4. Widely used in sample preparation for electron microscopy (e.g. in fixation buffers): it is perceived to preserve membrane ultrastructure and does not form the microprecipitation on thin sections that can occur with phosphate buffers if the specimen is not well rinsed between pre- and post-fixation. It also does not precipitate at low calcium concentrations, is easily prepared, and has a long shelf life (over a year, especially if refrigerated) as it does not support microbial growth. Caution: contains arsenic and is highly toxic.

Tris Buffers

Description

Tris buffers are useful in a biologically relevant range of pH 7.1–8.9. Tris is an organic compound with a zwitterionic structure that chelates metal ions and inactivates many enzymes (though it is still regarded as physiologically inert). It is widely used in chemistry and molecular biology, especially in agarose and SDS-PAGE gel electrophoresis (TBE, TAE, etc.), and has good buffering capacity at the higher pH required for some tissues. Note that the buffer pH is strongly temperature-dependent.

Formaldehyde

Description

Formaldehyde is one of the most-used fixatives for histopathology and immunohistochemistry. It penetrates tissue faster than glutaraldehyde or osmium tetroxide and reacts with proteins, unsaturated fatty acids and DNA, but preserves polysaccharides poorly and only partially destroys the osmotic properties of membranes (so osmolarity must be adjusted where possible). Fixative should be at ambient temperature or 37 °C to avoid disrupting the microtubule architecture of cells.

In time formaldehyde polymerises to paraformaldehyde (a white precipitate), so it must be stabilised for long-term storage. Formalin is formaldehyde in water (37–40%) with ~10–15% methanol to stabilise it; the methanol also fixes (denaturing proteins) and permeabilises membranes, which speeds penetration but makes formalin sub-optimal for GFP. Because fixation acidifies the solution it is usually buffered (neutral buffered formalin, NBF), which stores well at room temperature. For sensitive applications, methanol-free formaldehyde is prepared fresh from paraformaldehyde (which depolymerises around 65 °C); the working solution keeps for a few weeks at 4 °C (Helander 2000). Fixative solutions typically contain 2–8% formaldehyde.

“Paraformaldehyde” (PFA) is preferred for some protocols because commercial NBF contains methanol as an anti-polymerisation additive, whereas PFA is prepared fresh (or diluted from frozen / oxygen-free stock) and so avoids both the methanol and the formic-acid degradation that ages NBF. PFA is the formaldehyde fixative of choice for cardiac perfusions; short fixation of cryo-embedded tissue where antigen retrieval is to be avoided; in-situ hybridisation; and TEM (as an alternative to glutaraldehyde). MeOH-free single-use formaldehyde ampoules are now also available and are easier and safer to dilute than making solutions from powder.

Advantages

  1. Rapid rate of penetration (more rapid than either glutaraldehyde or osmium tetroxide).
  2. Reacts with proteins, unsaturated fatty acids and DNA. Very effective in fixing mucoproteins. Prevents extraction of glycogen if fixation is not prolonged.

Disadvantages

  1. Many reactions are reversible; for the most part the fixative is removed with washing.
  2. Only partially destroys the osmotic properties of membranes; osmolarity must be carefully adjusted when possible.
  3. Specimens cannot be stored for any length of time without further processing.
  4. Does not preserve soluble polysaccharides or acid mucopolysaccharides.

Glutaraldehyde

Description

Glutaraldehyde is a bifunctional aldehyde cross-linker widely used in biochemistry (SDS-PAGE) and as a tissue fixative, alone or mixed with formaldehyde/paraformaldehyde (see Glutaraldehyde–Formaldehyde). Enzyme activity is often preserved, allowing later cytochemical localisation. It penetrates faster than osmium tetroxide but slower than formaldehyde, and exerts some osmotic effect (usually negligible between 2–5%). It reacts with some lipids and can dissolve phospholipids (preventable with 1–3 mM CaCl2), and preserves high-molecular-weight DNA better than formaldehyde. It tends to deactivate proteins (e.g. quenching GFP), lowering immunoreactivity; glutaraldehyde-fixed tissue should be quenched with amine-containing molecules (ethanolamine, lysine, Tris-HCl) before IHC, or reduced with NaBH4.

Clean glutaraldehyde is a clear fluid that yellows as it polymerises; store in clean glass vials in the dark — stable for many months at −20 °C and may be kept at 4 °C. Typical protocols use 2–6%. In solution it polymerises and forms glutaric acid, causing gradual acidification (so buffering is needed; Sabatini et al. 1967). Polymerisation is rapid at high pH: ~50% of purified glutaraldehyde polymerises at 4 °C in seven weeks at pH 6.5, three weeks at pH 7.5 and six days at pH 8.5 — at pH ≥ 8.5 do not mix buffer and fixative until immediately before use. Glutaraldehyde is more stable in cacodylate buffers (Marquardt & Gordon).

Glutaraldehyde–Formaldehyde mixtures

Glutaraldehyde gives excellent ultrastructural preservation but penetrates slowly and can mask antigens; formaldehyde penetrates quickly but cross-links reversibly and preserves structure less well. Mixing the two combines the fast initial penetration of formaldehyde with the slower, stronger, irreversible cross-linking of glutaraldehyde — a good compromise for both light and electron microscopy. The classic example is Karnovsky’s fixative.

Recipes

2.5% Glutaraldehyde – 2% Paraformaldehyde (Karnovsky)

The two methods below both yield 10 ml of Karnovsky’s fixative (final 2.5% GA / 2% PFA). Method 1 combines two ready-made stocks; Method 2 mixes 25% glutaraldehyde directly with the PFA stock and buffer to save preparing the intermediate 5% GA stock.

Stock solutions

  • Stock A — 4% paraformaldehyde (PFA) in 0.1 M PBS or cacodylate buffer.
  • Stock B — 5% glutaraldehyde (GA) in 0.1 M buffer (25% glutaraldehyde 1 ml + 0.1 M buffer to 5 ml).

Method 1 — from 4% PFA and 5% GA stocks

Use this method if the intermediate 5% glutaraldehyde stock (Stock B) is already prepared.

ComponentAmount

  1. Label a clean glass vial or polypropylene tube “Karnovsky’s Fixative (2.5% GA / 2% PFA)” with the date and your initials.
  2. Pipette 5.0 ml of the 4% PFA stock (Stock A) into the tube.
  3. Pipette 5.0 ml of the 5% GA stock (Stock B) into the same tube.
  4. Cap and invert gently 5–10 times to mix homogeneously.
  5. Bring the solution to the required temperature (usually 4 °C or room temperature) before applying it to samples.

  • Do not pour leftovers down the sink — collect all waste in a designated aldehyde hazardous-waste container.

Glyoxal

Description

Glyoxal is a small two-carbon di-aldehyde (OHC–CHO) that can replace formaldehyde / paraformaldehyde (PFA) as a cross-linking fixative for immunofluorescence and immunohistochemistry. Like other aldehydes it fixes by cross-linking proteins, but its small size lets it penetrate tissue quickly and reach epitopes that PFA leaves masked. Richter et al. (2018) showed that glyoxal acts faster than PFA, cross-links proteins more effectively and improves the preservation of cellular morphology, while Konno et al. (2023) found that it greatly improves antibody penetration and immunoreactivity in brain tissue — uncovering signals for molecules buried within specialised neuronal compartments (e.g. ionotropic receptors at the postsynapse, voltage-gated channels at the axon initial segment) that 4% formaldehyde otherwise requires additional antigen-exposing steps to detect.

The working solution is an acidic mixture of glyoxal, acetic acid and ethanol; the low pH is part of the mechanism, and results are reported at both pH 4 and pH 5 (pH 5 giving the best morphology for cultured cells in Richter et al.). The solution is prepared fresh and is not stable for long-term storage.

Advantages

  1. Faster penetration and faster, more effective protein cross-linking than PFA.
  2. Improved antibody access — uncovers buried antigens that are masked by formaldehyde, often without a separate antigen-retrieval step.
  3. Better preservation of cellular morphology in cultured cells; enhances the immunosignal of most molecules.
  4. Formaldehyde-free, and less toxic / less hazardous by inhalation than PFA.

Notes & cautions

  1. Still an aldehyde — handle with gloves in a fume hood; the working solution is acidic.
  2. Prepare fresh, keep cool and use within a few days; glyoxal may precipitate on standing (redissolve a precipitated stock by warming to ~50 °C).
  3. The acidic, low-pH formulation can be unsuitable for pH-sensitive epitopes; choose pH 4 vs pH 5 (and acetic-acid content) for the application.

Methylane Blue / Trypan Blue

DESCRIPTION

Összevágva: Methylene Blue is a cationic stain (positively charged blue dye). It binds to negatively charged parts of the cells, such as nucleus (DNA) and RNA in the cytoplasm. It's a common stain for a large number of tasks including identification of bacteria, cellular structure in both plant and animal cells. It can be used to stain RNA/DNA in gels or RNA on hybridization membranes in northern blotting. The methylene blue is reduced in viable cells, leaving them unstained. However dead cells are unable to reduce the oxidized methylene blue and the cells are stained blue. Methylene blue can interfere with the respiration of the yeast as it picks up hydrogen ions made during the process.

Trypan blue is an azo dye originally used to dye cotton, first synthesized by Paul Ehrlich in 1904.. In biosciences it is used to analyze vitality of cells and tissue. Live cells with intact cell membranes are not coloured as the dye can not penetrate the plasma membrane. However it penetrates dead or apoptotic cells.

Cell Counting

Description

Counting cells on a haemocytometer under the microscope is a simple, low-cost and reliable way to measure cell concentration and viability, and remains one of the most widely used methods (Schisler 1986). We use a Neubauer-type chamber.

Adherent mammalian cells (trypsinisation)

Adherent mammalian cells must first be detached into a single-cell suspension before counting:

  1. Aspirate the medium and rinse the monolayer with Ca2+/Mg2+-free PBS to wash away residual serum (serum inhibits trypsin).
  2. Add a thin layer of 0.05–0.25% trypsin–EDTA and incubate at 37 °C for ~3–5 min until the cells round up and detach (tap the flask gently to help release).
  3. Neutralise the trypsin with an equal volume of serum-containing medium, then pipette up and down to break clumps into a single-cell suspension.
  4. Continue with the haemocytometer count below; trypan blue distinguishes live (unstained) from dead (blue) cells.

Protocol

You will need

Neubauer haemocytometer and coverslip, 70% ethanol, a single-cell suspension, 0.4% trypan blue, micropipettes and a microscope.

Procedure

  1. Clean the chamber and coverslip with 70% ethanol, then press the coverslip onto the chamber until Newton’s rings (rainbow fringes) appear — this sets the 0.1 mm chamber depth.
  2. Prepare a single-cell suspension and mix gently but thoroughly to break up clumps.
  3. Mix the suspension 1:1 with 0.4% trypan blue (dilution factor = 2) to stain dead cells and reach a countable density; count within ~3–5 min.
  4. Pipette ~10 µl to the edge of the coverslip and let capillary action fill the chamber in one motion; avoid bubbles and over- or under-filling.
  5. Let the cells settle (~1 min) and focus on the grid at 10× magnification.
  6. Count the four large corner squares (each 1 mm²). To avoid double counting, include cells touching the top and left boundary lines and exclude those touching the bottom and right. Live cells stay bright/refractile; dead cells take up the blue dye.
  7. Aim for 100–200 cells in total across the four squares; dilute and recount if the field is too crowded.

Calculation

Each large corner square (1 mm × 1 mm × 0.1 mm depth) holds 0.1 µl = 10−4 ml, so:

  • cells/ml = mean cells per large square × dilution factor × 104
  • total cells = cells/ml × suspension volume (ml)
  • viability (%) = viable cells ÷ total cells × 100

Before and after laboratory procedures,  work surfaces must be disinfected.

Sterilization of Equipment & Media

Sterilization Protocol

LOADING AUTOCLAVE:

Empty Containers:

  1. Fill blue cap glassware, vials, or containers to about 1/3 with ddH2O.
  2. Place cap on container BEWARE cap have to be loose.
  3. Cover the container top with aluminum foil and stick indicator tape on it.
  4. Place in an autoclave.

Liquids:

  1. Liquid levels can NOT be filled more than to 4/5-th of total volume.
  2. Place cap on container BEWARE cap have to be loose.
  3. Cover the container top with aluminum foil and stick indicator tape on it.
  4. Place in an autoclave.

Plasticware:

Tubes (eg. eppendorf tubes, storage tubes, caps): have to be placed into a secondary container and covered with aluminum foil. Stick indicator tape onto the aluminum foil.

Pipette tips: tips are placed into autoclavable boxes. Indicator tape is used to close the top.

STERILIZATION:

Reference Container:

  1. Take an empty container with the same volume and shape as the largest liquid body being sterilized and fill it with ddH2O to at least to the same level.
  2. Cover the mouth of the container with aluminum foil.
  3. Cover the cap with aluminum foil and stick indicator foil on it (as this water can be used as sterile water).
  4. Place both the container and cap into the autoclave.
  5. Stick the reference temperature probe into the container by piercing the aluminum foil.

Autoclave:

  1. Place the second temperature probe in the autoclave.
  2. Start the appropriate program on the autoclave.

RISKS

Do not autoclave containers containing bleach. The combination of bleach and residual cotton and oil (improperly cleaned autoclaves) may result in an explosive combustion within the autoclave.

eg. Cell culture media, pre-cultures, culture plates

Protocol:

  1. Chemically disinfect with a 1:10 final dilution (vol/vol) of household bleach. In case of multiwell plates fill plate withh 20% bleach.
  2. Swirl flask contents and allow a contact time of 30 minutes .
  3. Pour down a sink drain and flush the plumbing with an excess of water

Risks:

PHYSICAL DEACTIVATION:

Protocol:

  1. Place culture plates into autoclave bags facing up and stacked to avoid spilage/leakage. Containers with liquid media containing liquids that can be autoclaved can be placed in an autoclave with loosened tops. If needed use secondary containers eg. beakers for glass vial.
  2. Place autoclave bags and containers into autoclave but NOT in the basket but the standard bucket
  3. Start autoclave program that keeps contents for 30 minutes at 121°C and 200kPa

Storage

GENERAL CONSIDERATION:

Microbes are typically stored at 80°C Freezers. When freezing Glycerol (sometimes DMSO) is used, however optimal amount is not set. The ratio is typicaly 10% (eg ATCC)-20%.

STOCK SOLUTIONS:

40% v/v Glycerol (2X):

Container                        Media Volume

Glycerol                40ml

H2O         60ml

  1. Combine glycerol and water in a measuring tube.
  2. Sterilize:in autoclave (smaller volume can be filtered using 0.2um filter)

PROTOCOL:

  1. Prepare overnight preculture.
  2. Add 500 ul-s of the preculture with 500ul-s of the 40% v/v Glycerol Stock.
  3. Label vials and tops (be careful as some markers can not withstand -80°C

Recovery

PROTOCOL:

  1. Prepare appropriate media plates (typically 60mm Petri dishes).
  2. Remove frozen stock from freezer and put on ice. Take max 5-10 vials per turn.
  3. Use sterile toothpick or toothpick to streak on fresh plate.
  4. Check if label is legible and correct if needed

Recovery

GENERAL CONSIDERATION:

Always follow recommendations  (medium, atmospheric pressure, temperature) of the source of the strain and/or product description. Growth medium should be chosen to maximize the recovery of cells.  Rehydration is an important step and should not be omitted. Typically the first step is subculturing on liquid medium however it can be streaking onto solid medium. Below is a standard protocol.

Typically first subcultures start within 24-48 hours.

All manipulations should be carried out under the hood! Before starting to work disinfect working surfaces.

OPENING AMPULES:

Sterilize work surface before and after process.

  1. Disinfect the head of the ampules with alcohol-based disinfectant.
  2. Crack the ampule using cotton/fiberless cloth slightly.
  3. Open the ampule gently as small particles can be carried out so disinfect between strains.

PROTOCOL:

  1. To rehydrate freeze-dried strains, add 0.1 mL of culture medium.
  2. Mix well and transfer to a tube containing 2.5mL of medium.
  3. Incubate strains overnight or untilmilky and opaque.
  4. Cryopreserve (in 20% Glycerol) and/or streak onto plate.
